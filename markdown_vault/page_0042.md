THE DIFFERENT KINDS OF TRUSTS.

A trust may be either a simple or a discretionary one. In the former case, the trustees are bound to distribute the income or capital of the trust estate in accordance with the terms of the trust deed, without any discretion on their part. The distribution is thus determined by the settlor's directions and not subject to the trustees' judgment.

In the latter case, where a discretionary trust exists, the trustees have the power to exercise judgment in determining how the trust funds should be used or distributed among charitable objects as they see fit (b). Similarly, an advowson may be conveyed to trustees upon trust to elect and present a proper preacher (c), requiring the trustees to use their discretion in making such selections.

A mixture of trust and power is another form where the settlor outlines only the broad terms of the trust, leaving the details to be filled in by the trustees' good sense. The exercise of this power is mandatory, but the mode of its execution involves judgment (d).

This should not be confused with a common trust to which a power is annexed; in such cases, the trustees are bound to distribute the trust property as directed, and the manner of distribution is left open. However, in a mixture of trust and power, the trustees must act according to their discretion within the framework set by the settlor.

Examples include:
- A fund vested in trustees upon trust to distribute among such charitable objects as the trustees shall think fit (b).
- An advowson conveyed to them upon trust to elect and present a proper preacher (c).
