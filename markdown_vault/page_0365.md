resolution in Sir Moyle Finch's case was, that "a disseisor
was subject to no trust, nor any subpœna was maintainable
against him, not only because he was not in the post, but
because the right of inheritance or freehold was determinable at the common law, and not in Chancery, neither had
the cestui que trust (while he had his being) any remedy in
that case" (e).

And we may add the authority of Lord St.
Leonards, who, in his edition of Gilbert on Uses, observes,
"At this day every one is bound by a trust who obtains the
estate without a valuable consideration, or even for a valuable consideration if with notice, unless perhaps the lord by
escheat.
But persons claiming the legal estate by an actual
disseisin, without collusion with the trustee, will not be
bound by the trust.
Therefore, if I oust A., who is a trustee
for B. , and a claim is not made in due time, A. will be barred,
and his cestui que trust with him, although I had no notice
of the trust " (f) (1).

And the same thing may be inferred
from the terms of the section of the late Limitation Act
relating to express trusts (g).
